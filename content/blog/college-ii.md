These are the last few accounts of my frustration with the education system, now that I am leaving it the frustration that made me articulately describe the world I was in no longer comes naturally. And I'd rather have it this way. I hope that I never run into such a system or such people again, even if I do I hope it's a little better than before. 

### File work 

A lab notebook has to be maintained with the utmost sincerity. Describing your process of going from an experimental hypothesis to the results. Make notes of the theoretical concepts you shall apply and think are at play. Walk us through your process, taking readings quantifying the process variables to ensure all results are accounted for and replicable. Results must be objectively observed and quantified too, and recorded with the same detail. Don't forget to leave some safety instructions and precautions for the person that tries to replicate the experiment later on. 

Or alternatively, you could just pretend to do all that. You could walk into a lab that has all the "instruments" and means to do so, you can mark your attendance to prove that you were indeed there doing the experiment and copy the rest from someone that already did the dirty work long ago. The dirty work being the scribblings copied to a lab notebook. Lab notebooks are never made, they are inherited in India's long standing tradition of passing down knowledge. Someone some time had done those experiments. But the rules of science and laws of nature don't change do they ? Even when they do, you can always fudge up the numbers. Readings aren't meant to denote what you found, they're what you want them to be. 

The writeup is passed down, loosing information with each new generation. Information lost between two stages of rote copying  ranges from 5% to up to 70% of information loss. Myself being responsible for several instances of the loss of information edging towards the upper limit. File work, sometimes makes me feel as if I am here just to become a scribe.

### Attendance

If the UGC exists in the goodwill of all students then it must abolish the system of attendance entirely. From nowhere can it be proved that by merely attending 75 or 80% of the classes would make a student more competent. The fact is that classes simply do nothing for the student, given the quality of our classroom education. One argument to uphold stringent attendance norms, given to me by one of my professors was that placements are declining, more and more companies are hired less year by year. Why? Because students no longer attend their classes, so they have no knowledge of their core subjects. But reality begs to differ, and the difference between fantasy and reality is not far from the same professor's lecture hall. 

### Approvals

It has been more than once, that I have been denied a signature or a meaningless approval by a figure of authority just for them to soothe their own ego.  
The approval doesn't mean anything, it is merely a bureaucratic step in a series of meaningless bureaucratic steps. Regardless of that, authority will only be exercised in your favour if you are willing you have your nose rubbed into the ground. 

#### An honest man

The new head of the department had always seemed like a khadoos man that lives in his own world without a concern for what happens outside. A man who wouldn't care for your excuse and throw you into the never ending spiral of bureaucracy and make you relive Kafka's Trial. 

But when I went to him with a bunch of useless outdated paperwork that's so out of touch with reality that you wont even call it redundant, the HoD did something that I've never seen. He questioned his own authority, not in that "do you really think you deserve to bear the fruits of my authority" way that's been used to grant us approvals before. But in the "Why is this necessary ? and how am I in the position to authorize this?" way. 

He is in no conceivable way a man that would question the system, or try to reform it. But his out of touch-ness made him do something really cool, which I wouldn't have expected of him.
